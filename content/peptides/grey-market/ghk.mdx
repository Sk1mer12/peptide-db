## Background

GHK (Gly-His-Lys) is a naturally occurring tripeptide first isolated from human plasma albumin by Loren Pickart in 1973. It is found endogenously in plasma (200 ng/mL at age 20, declining to 80 ng/mL by age 60), saliva, and urine. GHK has extraordinarily high affinity for copper(II) ions (K_d ~ 10^-15 M), making it one of the strongest biological copper chelators known. The free tripeptide and its copper complex (GHK-Cu) are listed separately in this database; this entry covers the copper-free form.

## Distinction from GHK-Cu

GHK and GHK-Cu have overlapping but distinct pharmacology. The copper complex is the predominant form studied in wound healing and skin biology, but the free tripeptide retains significant biological activity via:
- Direct signalling through integrin and cell surface receptors
- Modulation of TGF-β, VEGF, and FGF expression
- Copper-independent anti-inflammatory effects (downregulates TNF-α, IL-6)
- Activation of proteasome activity and protein quality control

Some applications — particularly oral supplementation and certain injectable protocols — use the free tripeptide on the assumption that it chelates endogenous copper in vivo.

## Gene Expression Effects

Pickart's work and subsequent bioinformatics analyses have identified GHK as a broad transcriptomic modulator. High-throughput gene expression data from the Broad Institute Connectivity Map suggest GHK-stimulated gene expression patterns resemble those associated with younger tissue phenotypes, reversing signatures of genes upregulated in ageing, cancer, and inflammation. These findings are preliminary and correlation-based.

## Cosmetic Use

GHK is used as a cosmetic active (often without copper) in peptide serums, marketed for wound healing, anti-aging, and skin rejuvenation. Palmitoyl GHK (Pal-GHK) — the palmitoylated form — is used in Matrixyl 3000 combination products. Evidence at cosmetic concentrations is primarily in vitro and proprietary.
