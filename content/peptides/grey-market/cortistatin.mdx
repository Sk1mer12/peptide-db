## Discovery and Gene Distinction

Cortistatin was discovered in 1996 by Luis de Lecea at The Salk Institute as a brain-specific neuropeptide with structural homology to somatostatin. Despite sharing ~10 of 14 residues with SS-14 and binding to the same five somatostatin receptors (SSTR1–5), cortistatin is encoded by an entirely separate gene (CORT), expressed primarily in cortical and hippocampal interneurons, and has a distinct pharmacological profile.

## Key Pharmacological Differences from Somatostatin

The defining functional distinction of cortistatin is its endogenous activity at the ghrelin receptor (GHS-R1a) — an activity not shared by somatostatin. This means cortistatin has effects that cannot be replicated by somatostatin or its analogues. Documented unique effects include:

- **Slow-wave sleep induction** — ICV cortistatin administration in rats produces a clear increase in slow-wave and REM sleep that somatostatin does not
- **Cortical activity reduction** — produces hippocampal theta rhythm changes and cortical hyperpolarisation via potassium channel activation
- **Immune modulation** — anti-inflammatory effects in experimental arthritis and colitis models that differ mechanistically from somatostatin

## Isoforms

In humans, the primary active isoforms are:
- **Cortistatin-17** (CS-17) — the predominant form; a 17-amino acid cyclic peptide
- **Cortistatin-14** (CS-14) — shorter C-terminal form, analogous to SS-14
- **Cortistatin-29** (CS-29) — extended precursor form

## Research Applications

Cortistatin is used as a research tool in neuroscience (sleep-wake cycle research, cortical interneuron function), immunology (anti-inflammatory pathway studies), and endocrinology (GHS-R1a pharmacology alongside ghrelin and growth hormone secretagogues). Commercial research peptide suppliers sell synthetic CS-14 and CS-17 for laboratory use.
