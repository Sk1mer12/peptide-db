## Background

Vilon (Lys-Glu; KE) is a synthetic thymic dipeptide bioregulator developed by Vladimir Khavinson's group at the St. Petersburg Institute of Biogerontology. It is one of the simplest members of the Khavinson cytomax series — a dipeptide compared to the tetrapeptides Cortagen (AEDG) and Epithalon (AEDG analogue). It was derived from thymulin and other thymic polypeptides by progressive reduction to the minimum active sequence.

## Proposed Mechanism

Vilon is proposed to act as a thymic immune regulator by:
- Restoring T-lymphocyte differentiation in aged thymic tissue
- Modulating IL-2 and IL-6 cytokine secretion
- Inhibiting NF-κB-mediated inflammatory signalling
- Potentially interacting with regulatory gene promoter sequences

Russian preclinical studies also report anti-tumour effects in rodent carcinogenesis models, attributed to immune surveillance restoration.

## Evidence Base

The evidence is entirely from Russian-language preclinical and small observational studies. A key rodent study by Anisimov et al. (1994) reported reduced mammary tumour incidence with Vilon administration. Human data are limited to case series and uncontrolled observations published in Soviet/Russian journals. No peer-reviewed phase 1 or phase 2 trials have been published in Western indexed journals.

## Distinction from Thymalin

Thymalin is a polypeptide complex extracted from thymic tissue; Vilon is a fully synthetic dipeptide. Vilon represents Khavinson's effort to identify the minimal thymic active sequence. Both target thymic function, but Vilon's small size theoretically allows for oral bioavailability and more precise SAR analysis.
