## Background

Larazotide acetate (AT-1001) is a synthetic 8-amino acid peptide (sequence: Gly-Gly-Val-Leu-Val-Gln-Pro-Gly) derived from the receptor-binding domain of zonula occludens toxin (Zot), a protein produced by toxigenic strains of *Vibrio cholerae*. Paradoxically, while Zot opens tight junctions, larazotide acts as a competitive antagonist at the same receptor, thereby tightening epithelial barrier function.

## Mechanism

Larazotide acts locally in the gut lumen to antagonise the tight-junction opening activity of gliadin (gluten-derived peptides) and other permeability-disrupting stimuli. It stabilises the apical junctional complex — particularly occludin and zonulin-regulated pathways — reducing paracellular antigen flux. Because it acts locally with minimal systemic absorption, it is considered a gut-restricted therapeutic.

## Clinical Development

Phase 2 trials (2011–2013) demonstrated statistically significant reductions in intestinal permeability markers and improved GI symptoms in coeliac patients on a gluten-free diet exposed to deliberate gluten challenge. The most effective dose appeared to be 0.5 mg three times daily.

The pivotal Phase 3 trial (CeleSTE, NCT02642848) in patients with poorly controlled coeliac disease on a gluten-free diet failed to meet its primary endpoint of symptom reduction vs placebo. 9 Meters Biopharma subsequently deprioritised the programme.

## Grey-Market Use

Despite the failed Phase 3 result, larazotide continues to circulate in the biohacker and gut-health community, primarily among individuals with non-coeliac gluten sensitivity, SIBO, or leaky-gut-adjacent conditions. Evidence for these off-label uses is entirely anecdotal.
