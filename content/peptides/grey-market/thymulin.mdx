## Biology and Discovery

Thymulin (Facteur Thymique Sérique, FTS) is an endogenous nonapeptide hormone produced exclusively by thymic epithelial cells. It was first isolated by Jean-François Bach's group in France in the 1970s. Unlike Thymosin Alpha-1 (also in this database), thymulin is a small defined nonapeptide and its activity is absolutely dependent on zinc chelation — the zinc-free form (apothymulin) is biologically inert.

## Mechanism of Action

Thymulin promotes T-lymphocyte differentiation within the thymic environment: it induces the expression of T-cell surface markers (CD3, CD4, CD8), facilitates maturation of thymocytes into functional T-cells, and modulates cytokine production. Serum thymulin levels decline with age in parallel with thymic involution — levels peak in childhood and fall sharply after puberty, reaching near-zero in old age. This has made it a candidate target for immune senescence intervention.

## Relationship to Zinc Status

Because thymulin activity requires zinc binding, zinc deficiency is a cause of thymulin hypoactivity even when peptide levels are adequate. Administration of thymulin without adequate zinc is expected to be ineffective. Several studies have shown that zinc supplementation alone can restore thymulin activity in zinc-deficient elderly individuals.

## Distinction from Other Thymic Peptides

The database also contains:
- **Thymalin** — a polypeptide extract from thymic tissue (heterogeneous mixture)
- **Thymosin Alpha-1** — a 28-amino acid acetylated peptide from thymosin fraction 5
- **Vilon** — a synthetic Lys-Glu dipeptide (Khavinson series)

Thymulin is the only endogenous thymic nonapeptide hormone with a defined zinc-dependent mechanism and a characterised receptor interaction profile.
