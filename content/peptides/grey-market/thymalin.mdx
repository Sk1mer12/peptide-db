## Background

Thymalin is a polypeptide bioregulator extracted and purified from calf thymus glands, developed in the 1970s–1980s by Vladimir Khavinson and colleagues at the Russian Academy of Medical Sciences. It was registered in the USSR and subsequently Russia as *Timalin* for clinical use in immunodeficiency states. Unlike synthetic peptides, Thymalin is a complex mixture of low-molecular-weight polypeptides derived from thymic parenchyma.

## Mechanism

The proposed mechanism centres on restoration of thymic function and T-cell homeostasis in immune senescence. Thymalin is claimed to: promote T-lymphocyte differentiation and proliferation; restore CD4/CD8 ratios; enhance cytokine signalling (IL-2, interferon-γ); and exert anti-apoptotic effects on lymphoid tissue. The exact active peptide components have not been fully characterised by Western pharmacological standards.

## Russian Clinical Evidence

Thymalin has been used extensively in Russian clinical practice for:
- Postoperative immunodeficiency
- Chronic infectious disease with immune dysfunction
- Radiation-induced immune suppression
- Age-related immune decline (geroprotection)

Khavinson's group published longitudinal data in a cohort of elderly patients (Leningrad Veterans study) suggesting reduced mortality and infection rates with peptide bioregulator treatment, including Thymalin, compared to controls. These studies have not been replicated in independent Western trials.

## Peptide Bioregulator Framework

Thymalin is part of the Khavinson peptide bioregulator system alongside **Epithalon** (pineal), **Cortagen** (cardiac), **Vilon** (thymic dipeptide), and others — each targeted to a specific organ system. The theory holds that these tissue-specific peptides act as cytomax gene-expression regulators that restore youthful patterns of gene transcription in senescent cells.
