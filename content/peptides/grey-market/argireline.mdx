## Background

Argireline (INCI: Acetyl Hexapeptide-3, also registered as Acetyl Hexapeptide-8) is a synthetic hexapeptide with the sequence Ac-Glu-Glu-Met-Gln-Arg-Arg-NH2. It was developed by Lipotec (now Lubrizol Life Science) in Barcelona and introduced commercially around 2002 as a cosmetic active marketed as a topical alternative to botulinum toxin. It is the most commercially successful synthetic anti-wrinkle peptide in the cosmetics industry.

## Mechanism

Argireline's sequence mimics the N-terminal region of SNAP-25 (synaptosomal-associated protein 25), a component of the SNARE complex essential for acetylcholine vesicle fusion at the neuromuscular junction. The peptide competitively inhibits SNARE complex assembly, thereby attenuating acetylcholine release and reducing the intensity of facial muscle contraction. Unlike botulinum toxin, it does not cleave SNAP-25 — it competes for binding sites without causing irreversible proteolysis.

## Evidence

In vitro data and cell culture studies convincingly demonstrate SNARE disruption. A randomised controlled clinical study (Blanes-Mira, 2002) using a higher-molecular-weight precursor construct demonstrated 27% reduction in wrinkle depth vs vehicle after 30 days with a 10% concentration formulation. Independent confirmatory clinical data are limited; most efficacy claims in product marketing extrapolate from the original Lipotec data.

## Injectable Grey-Market Use

Some peptide vendors supply injectable-grade Argireline. Injectable use has no clinical evidence base, no safety data, and carries infection risk. This application is entirely experimental.

## SNAP-8 Relationship

SNAP-8 (Acetyl Glutamyl Heptapeptide-3) is an extended 8-amino acid version of Argireline with an additional Ala residue at the C-terminal, claimed to have greater SNARE affinity and reduced muscle activity.
