## Background

Buserelin (INN) is a synthetic nonapeptide analogue of gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH/LHRH) developed by Sanofi (formerly Hoechst) as HOE-766. It is a first-generation GnRH superagonist in the same pharmacological class as leuprolide, goserelin, and nafarelin. Buserelin has been approved and marketed in Europe (Suprefact), Canada (Suprefact), and numerous other countries since the 1980s but has never received FDA approval in the United States, where leuprolide (Lupron) dominates the equivalent market.

## Mechanism

Buserelin, like all GnRH agonists, initially stimulates pituitary LH and FSH secretion (the "flare" effect). With continuous administration, it causes receptor downregulation and pituitary desensitisation, resulting in profound suppression of LH, FSH, and consequently gonadal sex hormone production (testosterone in males, oestrogen in females) — a state of medical castration.

The D-Ser(tBu)6 substitution at position 6 and ethylamide at position 10 distinguish buserelin from native GnRH, conferring resistance to enzymatic degradation and 20- to 170-fold greater potency than native GnRH.

## Approved Indications (Europe/Canada)

- **Prostate cancer** — androgen deprivation therapy
- **Endometriosis** — oestrogen suppression
- **Uterine fibroids** — pre-surgical size reduction
- **Female infertility / IVF** — controlled ovarian stimulation protocols
- **Precocious puberty** — pubertal suppression

## Grey-Market Use in the US

US users without access to equivalent FDA-approved GnRH agonists (or seeking cheaper alternatives) obtain buserelin through grey-market peptide vendors. Uses include prostate cancer management, fertility protocols, and gender-affirming care. The absence of FDA approval creates regulatory ambiguity for importation.

## WADA Status

Buserelin is prohibited in-competition and out-of-competition as a GnRH analogue under WADA category S2 (Peptide Hormones, Growth Factors, Related Substances, and Mimetics).
