## Background

Cortagen is a synthetic tetrapeptide (Ala-Glu-Asp-Lys) developed within the Khavinson peptide bioregulator programme at the St. Petersburg Institute of Biogerontology. The programme postulates that each organ has a corresponding "cytomax" — a tissue-specific short peptide that, when administered exogenously, restores youthful patterns of gene expression in aged cells. Cortagen is designated the cardiac cytomax.

## Mechanism (Proposed)

Khavinson's group proposes that short di- and tetrapeptides interact directly with genomic DNA via complementary binding in promoter regions, stimulating transcription of genes associated with cell proliferation, differentiation, and stress resistance. For Cortagen, this is proposed to manifest as:
- Restoration of cardiomyocyte metabolic activity
- Reduction of fibrosis-associated gene expression
- Attenuation of cardiac remodelling in aged tissue

These mechanisms remain speculative by Western pharmacological standards and have not been validated in peer-reviewed large-animal or human studies outside the Russian biogerontology literature.

## Evidence Status

Evidence consists entirely of Russian-language preclinical studies, cell culture experiments, and small uncontrolled human cohorts. No placebo-controlled RCTs have been published in indexed Western journals. Computational studies have explored potential peptide-DNA interactions. Independent replication is lacking.

## Context within Khavinson Framework

Cortagen sits alongside Thymalin (thymus), Epithalon (pineal gland/telomere), Vilon (thymic dipeptide), and Livagen (liver) as part of a coherent Russian biogerontological system. The programme has generated a substantial body of internal literature but remains outside mainstream Western pharmacology.
