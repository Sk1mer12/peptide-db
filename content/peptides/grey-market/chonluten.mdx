## Background

Chonluten is a Gly-Glu-Pro tripeptide belonging to Vladimir Khavinson's series of tissue-specific short peptide bioregulators developed at the St. Petersburg Institute of Biogerontology. The Khavinson group proposed that each organ or tissue system contains a specific regulatory peptide that can be identified by fractionating tissue extracts to their minimal active sequence. For bronchial tissue, the identified minimal sequence was Gly-Glu-Pro.

## Proposed Mechanism

The Khavinson model holds that short peptide bioregulators interact directly with gene promoter elements (specifically TATA-box and related regulatory sequences) to modulate transcription in a tissue-specific manner. Chonluten is proposed to influence bronchial epithelial cell proliferation, mucosal cytokine secretion, and respiratory immune responses. Animal studies cited by the Khavinson group report reduced histological damage in aged bronchial tissue and changes in epithelial cell differentiation markers.

## Evidence Base

Evidence is limited to Russian-language preclinical publications and uncontrolled observational data. No randomised controlled trials have been conducted in any jurisdiction. The tripeptide's very small size suggests it is likely degraded in the GI tract, casting doubt on sublingual bioavailability claims. No independent Western replication of the proposed mechanism exists.

## Relationship to Other Khavinson Peptides

Chonluten is part of the Cytomax/Cytamins family that includes Epitalon (AEDG, pineal), Vilon (KE, thymus), Cortagen (AEDL, cardiac), Pinealon (EDR, brain), Testagen (KEDV, testicular), and Vesugen (KED, vascular). Each is proposed to be tissue-specific based on the source tissue from which it was originally derived.
