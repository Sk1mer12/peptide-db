## Background

Sigumir is a cartilage-derived polypeptide bioregulator from Vladimir Khavinson's series. Like Thymalin (thymus) and Cortagen (cardiac tissue), Sigumir is the polypeptide complex form — derived directly from animal cartilage — as opposed to a fully synthetic defined-sequence peptide. The Khavinson group identified the minimal active synthetic sequence from cartilage fractions as Ala-Glu-Asp (the tripeptide marketed separately as Cartalax).

## Polypeptide vs Synthetic Distinction

Sigumir (polypeptide complex) and Cartalax (Ala-Glu-Asp synthetic tripeptide) are sometimes used interchangeably by vendors but represent different products:
- **Sigumir** — a heterogeneous polypeptide extract retaining a mixture of regulatory peptides from cartilage tissue
- **Cartalax** — the fully synthetic minimal-sequence tripeptide equivalent (Ala-Glu-Asp)

The polypeptide complex form may contain additional bioactive sequences beyond the defined tripeptide.

## Proposed Uses

Sigumir is proposed for age-related cartilage deterioration, osteoarthritis, and joint function decline. Animal studies from the Khavinson group report improved chondrocyte histology and proteoglycan content in aged rodent cartilage after treatment. Human data are limited to uncontrolled observational reports in Russian-language publications.

## Evidence Status

No randomised placebo-controlled trials have been published in indexed English-language literature. The broader evidence base for Khavinson's polypeptide complexes is limited by the same methodological concerns that apply to the entire series: small samples, no blinding, and publication exclusively in Russian biogerontology journals without independent replication.
