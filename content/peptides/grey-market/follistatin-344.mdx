## Biology and Isoform Context

Follistatin is an endogenous glycoprotein produced by many tissues, primarily known for binding and neutralising TGF-β superfamily members including myostatin (GDF-8), activin A, activin B, and GDF-11. Three primary isoforms arise from alternative splicing and proteolytic processing of the FST gene: FS-288, FS-315, and FS-344. FS-344 is the predominant circulating isoform and differs from FS-315 by a 27-amino acid C-terminal extension (the EI domain) that contains a heparin-binding motif.

## FS-344 vs FS-315

The heparin-binding domain in FS-344 causes it to associate strongly with heparan sulfate proteoglycans on cell surfaces and in the extracellular matrix. This means FS-344 acts more locally and is sequestered to the tissue of injection, whereas FS-315 distributes more systemically. In rodent studies, intramuscular injection of FS-344 produces localised hypertrophy of the injected muscle without significant systemic effects on reproductive organs — a property that has made it the preferred isoform in preclinical muscle research.

## Preclinical Evidence

Gene delivery studies (AAV-follistatin) in mice and non-human primates have demonstrated dramatic muscle hypertrophy (up to 200% above baseline in knockout models). Protein administration studies show more modest effects. Duchenne muscular dystrophy mouse models treated with follistatin-344 showed improved strength and histology.

## Human Context

No phase 1 safety trials of injected follistatin protein have been completed or published. The compound is widely available from grey-market research vendors. The risks of exogenous activin/myostatin inhibition in humans — including effects on cardiac remodelling, reproductive hormones, and oncogenesis — are uncharacterised.
