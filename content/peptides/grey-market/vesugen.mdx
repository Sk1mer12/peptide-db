## Background

Vesugen (Lys-Glu-Asp; KED) is a synthetic tripeptide bioregulator derived by Vladimir Khavinson's group from vascular tissue fractions. The compound targets endothelial function and is proposed to counteract age-related vascular stiffening, endothelial dysfunction, and atherosclerosis progression. It is part of the Khavinson Cytomax series of organ-specific regulatory peptides.

## Proposed Mechanism

Within the Khavinson theoretical framework, Vesugen is proposed to enter vascular endothelial cells and directly interact with TATA-box and CAAT-box regulatory elements in gene promoters, selectively upregulating genes associated with endothelial health, nitric oxide synthase activity, and vascular wall repair. Animal studies published by the Khavinson group report improved endothelial histology and reduced markers of vascular aging in rodent models.

## Structural Similarity to Other Khavinson Peptides

Vesugen (KED) shares two of its three residues with Testagen (KEDV), differing only by the absence of the C-terminal valine. This structural overlap raises questions about selectivity claims. The Khavinson group argues that even single amino acid differences confer organ specificity, a claim not independently validated.

## Evidence Assessment

No peer-reviewed, placebo-controlled human trials have been published outside of Russian biogerontology journals. Independent replication by Western research groups is absent. Bioavailability by sublingual or subcutaneous routes has not been rigorously characterised in pharmacokinetic studies in indexed journals.
