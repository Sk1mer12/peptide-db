## Background

Gonadorelin is the synthetic form of native gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH), a decapeptide synthesised and secreted in a pulsatile manner by hypothalamic neurons. It was first isolated and sequenced by Andrew Schally (1971 Nobel Prize). Its physiological role is to drive pulsatile LH and FSH release from pituitary gonadotrophs, which in turn govern sex steroid production and gametogenesis.

## Historical FDA Approval

Two gonadorelin products received FDA approval:
- **Factrel** (Gonadorelin HCl) — approved for diagnosing hypogonadotropic hypogonadism and testing pituitary GnRH reserve
- **Lutrepulse** (Gonadorelin acetate) — approved for infertility treatment in women with hypothalamic amenorrhoea, delivered via pulsatile IV pump

Both have been withdrawn from the US market. No pharmaceutical company currently manufactures gonadorelin for human use in the United States. It remains available in the EU and UK (Fertagyl, HRF) and as a veterinary product (Cystorelin, OvaCyst) for synchronising oestrus in cattle.

## Research Context and Current Use

Despite commercial discontinuation for human use, gonadorelin is widely used in endocrine research as a reference standard and as a pituitary stimulation test reagent. In reproductive medicine, it is occasionally compounded in the US for fertility applications. Research peptide vendors sell it for in vitro and animal studies.

## Distinction from GnRH Analogues

The database also contains GnRH agonists (Leuprolide, Goserelin, Buserelin, Triptorelin, Histrelin, Nafarelin) and antagonists (Cetrorelix, Degarelix, Ganirelix). These are synthetic analogues with modified pharmacokinetics. Gonadorelin itself is the native decapeptide with a 2–10 minute half-life and is the only form capable of producing physiological pulsatile responses when delivered via pump.
