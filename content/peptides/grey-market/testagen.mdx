## Background

Testagen (Lys-Glu-Asp-Val; KEDV) is a synthetic tetrapeptide developed by Vladimir Khavinson's group at the St. Petersburg Institute of Biogerontology as a tissue-specific bioregulator for testicular function. It was derived from fractionation of testicular tissue extracts to identify a minimal active regulatory sequence. Within the Khavinson framework, each peptide is considered to act specifically on the tissue from which it was derived.

## Proposed Action

Testagen is proposed to modulate Leydig cell function, support testosterone biosynthesis, and counteract age-related decline in testicular function. The theoretical mechanism, consistent across Khavinson's entire peptide series, involves direct interaction with gene promoter regulatory sequences (particularly TATA-box homology regions) to influence transcription of tissue-specific genes.

## Evidence Base

Published evidence consists of Russian-language preclinical studies in rodents and a small number of uncontrolled human observations. No peer-reviewed, placebo-controlled clinical trials have been conducted in Western indexed journals. The proposed mechanism of TATA-box interaction for short tetrapeptides remains highly speculative and has not been independently validated. Given the peptide's small size and lack of oral stability data, bioavailability by the sublingual route is uncertain.

## Context in the Khavinson Series

Testagen is part of the broader Khavinson cytomax series alongside Epitalon (AEDG, pineal), Cortagen (AEDL, cardiac), Vilon (KE, thymic), Chonluten (GEP, bronchial), Vesugen (KED, vascular), and Pinealon (EDR, brain).
