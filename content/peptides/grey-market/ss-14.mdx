## Biology

Somatostatin-14 is the primary cyclic form of endogenous somatostatin, discovered in 1973 by Brazeau et al. in Guillemin's laboratory while searching for a growth hormone-releasing factor. It acts via five G-protein coupled receptors (SSTR1–5) that are widely distributed across the CNS, pituitary, pancreas, and GI tract. The native peptide contains an obligatory Cys₃–Cys₁₄ disulfide bridge that forms the 12-residue ring essential for receptor binding.

## SS-14 vs SS-28

Two native somatostatin forms exist:
- **SS-14** — the C-terminal 14-amino acid form, processed from prosomatostatin; predominant in the hypothalamus and brain
- **SS-28** — the extended 28-amino acid form with an N-terminal extension; predominant in gut and pancreas; somewhat higher potency at SSTR5

Both are degraded within minutes by plasma and tissue endopeptidases. Their ultra-short half-lives drove the development of stable analogues.

## Synthetic Analogues in This Database

The approved synthetic somatostatin analogues — **Octreotide** (Sandostatin), **Lanreotide** (Somatuline), and **Pasireotide** (Signifor) — were developed specifically because of SS-14's rapid degradation. These compounds incorporate D-amino acids and modified ring structures to extend half-life to hours or days while retaining preferential binding to SSTR2 (octreotide, lanreotide) or pan-SSTR activity (pasireotide).

## Research Use

SS-14 is used primarily as:
- A reference agonist in receptor binding and functional assays for all five SSTR subtypes
- A tool compound to study GH suppression and pancreatic hormone regulation in animal models
- A comparator in structure-activity relationship studies for novel somatostatin analogues

It is not a practical therapeutic agent due to its extreme instability in vivo.
