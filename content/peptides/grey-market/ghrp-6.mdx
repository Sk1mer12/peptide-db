## Background and Discovery

GHRP-6 (His-D-Trp-Ala-Trp-D-Phe-Lys-NH₂) was developed in the 1980s by Cyril Bowers at Tulane University as part of a systematic effort to create synthetic peptides capable of stimulating GH release independently of GHRH. It was among the first compounds to define the GHS-R1a (ghrelin) receptor class, years before ghrelin itself was discovered. GHRP-6 is a hexapeptide with two D-amino acid substitutions (D-Trp at position 2, D-Phe at position 5) that confer resistance to aminopeptidase degradation.

## Mechanism of Action

GHRP-6 acts via two complementary mechanisms:
1. **GHS-R1a agonism** — stimulates pulsatile GH release from pituitary somatotrophs; acts synergistically with GHRH
2. **Hypothalamic ghrelin receptor activation** — strongly suppresses somatostatin tone while potently stimulating appetite via vagal afferents and hypothalamic NPY/AgRP neurons

The appetite-stimulatory effect is markedly stronger with GHRP-6 than with later GHRPs (GHRP-2, ipamorelin, hexarelin), making it the compound of choice when both GH release and caloric intake are desired — but a disadvantage for body composition-focused protocols.

GHRP-6 reaches the pituitary via blood and also via intranasal routes (which bypass the blood-brain barrier via the olfactory pathway), explaining the clinical utility of intranasal administration.

## Regulatory Status

FDA included GHRP-6 in its list of bulk drug substances that raise significant safety concerns and cannot be used in compounding. No therapeutic product based on GHRP-6 has been approved anywhere. WADA classifies GHRP-6 as a growth hormone secretagogue prohibited in and out of competition.
