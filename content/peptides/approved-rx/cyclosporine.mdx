## Background

Cyclosporine A is a cyclic undecapeptide produced by the soil fungus *Tolypocladium inflatum*, first isolated in Norway in 1970. Its immunosuppressive properties were discovered by Jean Borel at Sandoz in 1972. FDA approved in 1983 for organ transplantation, cyclosporine transformed the field — dramatically improving graft survival rates and enabling routine kidney, heart, and liver transplantation.

## Mechanism

Cyclosporine binds cyclophilin (a cytoplasmic immunophilin) to form a complex that inhibits calcineurin phosphatase. Calcineurin normally dephosphorylates NFAT (nuclear factor of activated T cells), enabling its nuclear translocation and IL-2 gene transcription. By blocking this step, cyclosporine prevents T-cell activation and proliferation.

## Approved Indications

- **Solid organ transplant rejection prevention** (kidney, liver, heart)
- **Rheumatoid arthritis** (severe, active)
- **Psoriasis** (severe, recalcitrant)
- **Dry eye disease** (Restasis/Cequa — ophthalmic emulsion, 0.05–0.09%)

## Pharmacokinetics and Monitoring

Cyclosporine has highly variable oral bioavailability (20–50%) and requires trough level monitoring (target varies by indication and time post-transplant). Neoral (microemulsion) provides more consistent absorption than Sandimmune (oil solution). CYP3A4 is the primary metabolic enzyme — extensive drug interactions.
