## Background

B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP) is synthesised and released by ventricular cardiomyocytes in response to increased myocardial wall stress. Nesiritide (Natrecor) is identical in structure to endogenous human BNP-32 and was FDA-approved in August 2001 for dyspnoea relief in acute decompensated heart failure.

## Mechanism

Nesiritide binds natriuretic peptide receptor-A (NPR-A) on vascular smooth muscle and endothelial cells, activating guanylyl cyclase and increasing intracellular cGMP. This produces arterial and venous vasodilation (reducing preload and afterload) and natriuresis/diuresis via renal tubular effects.

## Clinical Controversy

Initial enthusiasm was tempered by a 2005 meta-analysis suggesting increased worsening renal function and 30-day mortality. The definitive ASCEND-HF trial (2011, n=7,141) showed nesiritide modestly improved dyspnoea but did not reduce 30-day mortality or rehospitalisation, and did not significantly worsen renal function. Use has declined markedly in favour of diuretics and vasodilators with clearer benefit profiles.

## Current Role

Nesiritide remains approved but is used infrequently. Current AHA/ACC guidelines do not recommend it for routine use in acute decompensated HF due to limited incremental benefit over standard therapy.
