## Background

ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone) is a 39-amino acid peptide secreted by the anterior pituitary that stimulates adrenal cortisol synthesis. Cosyntropin is the biologically active N-terminal 24-amino acid fragment of ACTH, first synthesised in the 1960s. Because the first 24 residues contain full corticotropic activity, cosyntropin is equipotent to native ACTH for adrenal stimulation.

## Diagnostic Use

The **cosyntropin stimulation test** (also called the "cortrosyn test" or "short Synacthen test") is the standard diagnostic test for adrenal insufficiency:

1. Baseline serum cortisol is measured
2. 250 µg cosyntropin is administered IV or IM
3. Serum cortisol is measured at 30 and 60 minutes

A peak cortisol ≥18–20 µg/dL (>500 nmol/L) is considered a normal (sufficient) response. Blunted response indicates adrenal insufficiency.

## Primary vs Secondary AI

Primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease — adrenal gland failure) produces low cortisol with high ACTH; cosyntropin fails to stimulate cortisol. Secondary AI (pituitary/hypothalamic failure) can produce a borderline low-dose test result but may show adequate response at higher doses; the 1 µg low-dose test is more sensitive for secondary AI.

## WADA Status

ACTH and cosyntropin are prohibited in competition (S2 Peptide Hormones) due to potential for enhancing cortisol production and masking fatigue, though their use in sport is rare and primarily a concern in ultra-endurance events.
